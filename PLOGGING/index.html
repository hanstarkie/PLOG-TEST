Plogging: Picking up a new Scandinavian trend

We tried Sweden's newest lifetyle trend in Sheffield

While ‘lagom’ brought us life balancing stress-relief, and ‘hygge’ offered us an excuse for cosy jumpers and hot chocolates, the Swedish ‘plogging’ trend is a far stride from comfort and nostalgia - something we find out standing outside in our gym gear on a freezing February day, rubbish bags in hand.

Plogging is the latest Scandinavian wellbeing craze to sweep the nation. But this time, it’s more hard graft and less winding down. Originating in a country renowned for its social conscience, the exercise combines litter picking and jogging into an alternative workout to clean up our parks and streets whilst keeping fit.

Last month, GoodGym brought the trend to Sheffield, with a growing number of runners taking part in their ‘plogs’, led by trainer, Tom Mutton.

The organisation - which stretches to 39 areas so far in the UK, with a further 69 areas in planning - holds mission runs, coach runs and group runs. These help the local community with one-off tasks such as gardening or moving furniture whilst on their jog, as well as incorporating visits to the elderly within their runs, to help combat loneliness in the area. But plogging is GoodGym’s newest mission and emphasises its ‘Do Good, Get Fit’ ethos.

This isn’t to say that the activity was non-existent before a label was attached to it. Runners Against Rubbish, which was founded by University of Sheffield research associate Stuart Walker, has been going since 2016 and encourages all runners to pick up any litter they see when out running. “Whenever anybody is going out for a run we say obviously don’t drop litter and if you see the odd couple of little bits along the side of the track we ask people to pick it up,” says Walker.

Litter in Sheffield

While many in Sheffield are green-thinking, others still haven’t received the memo. From June 2016 to June 2017, Sheffield City Council issued 656 notices to litter droppers ordering them to pay a standard £80 fine. Those who refused to pay the fine were taken to court which resulted in 194 people being convicted at Sheffield Magistrates court.

But, as even the Prime Minister recently unveiled a 25-year environmental plan to reduce plastic waste, has there ever been a better time to plog? We thought not and decided to give it a go.

We chucked on our leggings, grabbed a bin bag and headed to Showroom cinema to meet a bunch of people who had volunteered to spend their Wednesday lunch break plogging. Fortunately, we were lent a pair of gloves each by the coach. Then it was straight into it, past the station and up Park Hill.

Unbeknownst to us beforehand, part of the Good Gym programme, includes box jumps up the Park Hill amphitheatre at the halfway point of the run - as if the combination of picking up litter and running at a sprint pace wasn’t enough high intensity.

Still, it’s satisfying to run back down through visibly cleaner streets that were once home to plastic bottles, wrappers, and old pizza boxes and there’s something inspiring about being surrounded by a bunch of people who happily do this on a weekly basis and not for a piece of investigative journalism.

By the end, our verdict on plogging was unanimous. While we can’t say we’ll be back there freezing our fingers off every Monday, the experience was definitely worthwhile.

Plogging, just like the other Scandi trends us Brits have fallen for, leaves you with a feeling of inner calm after. And, if you’re going to jump on a hashtag bandwagon - what better else is there than to combine selflessness with being on trend?
